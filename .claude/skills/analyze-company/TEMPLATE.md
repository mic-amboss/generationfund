# [Company Name] — Deep Dive

---

## Overview

A concise portrait of the company in 2-3 paragraphs. What does it do, how big is it, and why is it interesting? Set the stage — a reader with zero context should be oriented after this section.

Include: Rough overview of the company, it's business model, market position, and the one-sentence thesis for why this company deserves attention.

---

## History

The company's story as a narrative, not a timeline. Focus on the inflection points — founding, key leadership transitions, strategic pivots, transformative acquisitions, and crises. What decisions shaped what the company is today?

Organize chronologically but spend more space on the eras that matter most. A company's founding story and its last decade usually deserve more ink than the middle years.

---

## Business Model & Market Position

### Business Model

Products, revenue streams, customer types, unit economics, and the basic mechanics of the business. Be specific - how does the company actually make money?

### Industry & competitive landscape

Market size, growth drivers, fragmentation, and where this company sits. Who are the real competitors and how does this company differentiate? How does the industry work, where does value creation happen?

### Competitive advantages

What structural advantages does this company have? Think about scale, network effects, switching costs, brand, regulatory moats, distribution advantages, or proprietary technology. Be specific - how exactly does the competitive advantage work in this case? Be honest — not every company has a moat. If the advantage is thin, say so.

### Financial profile

Key financial characteristics: revenue growth trajectory, margin profile, capital intensity, return on invested capital, cash conversion, and capital allocation track record. Use 5-year trends where available. A simple table works well here.

## Key People & Corporate Culture

### Leadership portraits

Detailed profiles of the 5-10 people who matter most — CEO, founder, chairman, key executives, influential board members. For each person:
- Background and path to the company
- Leadership style and philosophy (use quotes from transcripts and letters)
- Key decisions they've made and their impact
- Compensation and skin in the game
- How they're perceived by investors and analysts

These portraits should be vivid enough that the reader gets a sense of the person, not just their resume.

### Corporate culture

What is it like to work here? How does the company describe its own culture, and does the evidence support it? Look for clues in employee reviews, management commentary, compensation structure, and turnover.

### Incentive structure

How are executives and employees compensated? What behaviors do the incentives encourage? Any unusual arrangements (long-term share grants, mandatory stock ownership, profit sharing, deferred compensation)? The incentive structure often reveals more about a company's true priorities than any mission statement.

## Ownership & Transactions

### Insider ownership

Who has meaningful skin in the game? Founders, family members, executives, and directors with significant stakes. How has insider ownership changed over time? Any notable buying or selling?

### Institutional & notable investors

Which long-term fundamental investors hold positions? What's their thesis? Summarize key points from fund letters and analysis.

### Key transactions

Significant acquisitions, divestitures, or corporate actions that shaped the company. For each: what was acquired, when, for how much, and how did it work out?

## Unique Insights

This is the most important section. What makes this company genuinely different? Not marketing-speak differentiators, but the real, non-obvious things you'd only learn by reading the primary sources carefully.

Examples of what belongs here:
- Unusual governance or compensation arrangements
- Cultural practices that are rare in the industry
- Strategic decisions that go against conventional wisdom
- Founder/family dynamics that shape the business
- Hidden assets or optionality that aren't obvious from the financials
- Management quirks or philosophies that influence operations
- Anything that made you think "huh, that's interesting"

Each item should be a mini-story: what it is, why it matters, and what it tells you about the company.

---

## Open Questions

What couldn't be answered from the available sources? What would you want to ask management? What deserves further investigation?

This section is honest about gaps — better to name them than to paper over them with confident-sounding speculation.
